Narratives

Disaster Damage:

On September 14, 2018 Hurricane Florence made landfall near Wrightsville Beach in New Hanover County, North Carolina. In the days prior to landfall, Florence had exhibited wind speeds typical of a Category 4 Hurricane but was downgraded to Category 1 before eventual landfall Despite the downgrade in intensity, Florence inundated parts of North Carolina and was the wettest tropical cyclone in the history of the Carolinas with rainfall totals greater than 25 - 35 inches in parts of the State. The rainfall intensity, combined with the slow-moving southwest track of the system and large wind field contributed to historic flooding across Southeastern and Central North Carolina. The rainfall fed the Cape Fear, Lumberton, and Waccamaw Rivers and lead to intense riverine flooding, damaging infrastructure, homes, and businesses in the surrounding area. More than nine river gauges registered flood conditions greater than a 500year event. The majority of damage caused by Hurricane Florence is due to this extended rainfall as the storm trekked southwest slowly through coastal North Carolina for six days. In addition to rainfall, Florence drove a record-breaking storm surge of 9 – 13 feet. The result of the storm surge, rainfall, and river overflow was catastrophic and life-threatening floods for a massive geographical extent of the State.

Recovery Needs:

The State of North Carolina consulted multiple resources to better understand the unmet recovery needs relative to housing, infrastructure, and economic revitalization following the catastrophic impacts of Hurricane Florence. The following analysis identifies the effects, longterm recovery needs, and recovery priorities relative to the CDBG-DR allocation provided to the State to perform disaster recovery activities. The unmet recovery needs analysis considers the impacts of DR – 4393, the presidentially-declared disaster designation for Hurricane Florence. Where feasible, recovery programs are taken in context with the ongoing recovery needs relative to the impacts of Hurricane Matthew to find efficiencies in planning, program design, and eventual program implementation. The foundation of the analysis of the unmet recovery need concerning housing is the State of North Carolina Housing Impact Assessment, completed by the Federal Housing Recovery Support Team (RSF) in coordination with HUD on March 12, 2019. The results of the Housing Impact Assessment were compared to the requirements set forth in 85 FR 4681 and previous Federal Register Notices. Where necessary, the Housing Impact Assessment assumptions were built upon or modified to better meet HUD guidance on the unmet housing recovery need methodology set therein. Other data sources were necessary to complete the analysis of unmet infrastructure recovery needs and the analysis of economic impacts from the storm. The analysis is based on data provided by state and federal agencies, impacted areas, local nonprofits and other stakeholders, the U.S. Census Bureau, the U.S. Small Business Administration (SBA), The Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA), and other sources. The North Carolina State Office of Budget and Management (NCOSBM) drafted multiple reports in October 2018 that cataloged initial impacts and provided an estimate of the unmet recovery needs at that time. This groundwork was necessary to develop the current unmet recovery needs analysis. The full NCOSBM report is available at https://files.nc.gov/ncosbm/documents/files/Florence_Report_Full_rev20181016v10.pdf (opens in new window). The analysis includes details specific to the HUD-identified most impacted and distressed (MID) areas as well as other impacted areas. The analysis provides details on the assistance received to date, catalogs any pre-existing challenges these impacted communities face, and provides the foundation for delivering recovery programming that seeks to cure the effects of the disaster while also preparing North Carolina for future disaster events. NCORR is charged with administering CDBG-DR funds as Grantee to HUD. Therefore, NCORR has sought to develop an unmet needs analysis which is true to the conditions in the State using the best available data and resources to help inform the disaster recovery. NCORR understands that future information may become available that would adjust the findings of the unmet needs analysis. Changes to the Action Plan may result if additional funds become available or if new information is discovered during program planning, development, and delivery that informs a more beneficial recovery.

Overall Progress Narrative:

For the quarter, the Homeowner Recovery Program (HRP) has completed the construction of 22 for low and moderate income beneficiaries, 10 urgent need beneficiaries, of which 4 elevated homes included construction complete. Of the total completed construction activities, 6 low and moderate income beneficiaries for Robeson County have been completed thus far.

The Affordable Housing Development Fund had 3 activity changes during the quarter. These projects obtained their Authority to Use Grant Funds (AUGF) from HUD recently and completed their Environmental Reviews. With the Environmental Reviews completed by the end of Q1, the Five Points project developer staged a groundbreaking ceremony in Q2. An approved Planning activity consisting of a storm water drainage study has achieved more than 50% completion and should complete in Q3 2022. Infrastructure also received, processed, and reimbursed $190,458.00 for this planning activity during Q2 2022. The Development Finance Initiative at the UNC School of Government completed and billed for milestones in their planning project. During the reporting period, ReBuild NC programming continued to implement recovery programs in accordance with fair housing requirements and best practices. NCORR established a new Fair Housing and Reasonable Accommodations role full-time to address reasonable accommodations, fair housing considerations, and improving fair housing policies throughout NCORR programming. Through such new role, ongoing recovery program policies and procedures were reviewed to ensure continued implementation of best practices and regulatory obligations. As a result of such efforts, an updated Reasonable Accommodation Policy was drafted during the reporting period.

The NCORR Compliance Team provided in person and remote technical assistance for internal and external recipients of HUD grant funds and was completed within this Q2 timeframe. Additional documentation for all technical assistance and monitoring was previously provided via individual reports. Technical assistance on cross-cutting regulations were provided on the following topics: Section 3 and Fair Housing. NCORR Compliance Team partnered with the Section 3 Coordinator and DRGR team to provide Section 3 technical assistance for NCORR program managers and staff in early April and to Subrecipient, partner agencies, and contractors in late April. NCORR is working with subrecipients for Section 3 Plan adoption and to ensure the project implementation plan (PIP) is part of current bid packets. NCORR provided training to HRP contractors in June to ensure they understand the new rule and are working toward achieving benchmarks. NCORR will continue to collect, review, and retain Section 3 reports in effect under the old rule. Geographical dispersion of housing projects throughout Most Impacted and Distress (MID) communities, as well as out of state construction of manufactured and modular housing will render Targeted Section 3 benchmarks difficult to attain. Fair Housing technical assistance was given to NCORR internal staff on the Fair Housing Act, Civil Rights Laws, and information regarding the requirement to further fair housing.

Four sub recipients benefited from an onsite technical assistance on how to prepare of monitoring and audits. This allowed subrecipients to learn how to become more efficient and organized for future monitoring or audits. . During the Rebuild training for new staff, compliance provided training on Fraud, Waste, and Abuse. NCORR Monitoring and Compliance Department completed one full monitoring review of a sub recipient of HUD funding. Monitoring allows the Compliance Team to review financial management, procurement, and other items as necessary. Even though no findings or concerns were given. The Compliance Team will continue to provide technical assistance for sub recipients to maintain knowledge and expectations for federal funding.
